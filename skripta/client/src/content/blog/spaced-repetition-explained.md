If you've ever aced a quiz the day after cramming and then drawn a total blank on the same material two weeks later, you've already experienced the core problem spaced repetition is designed to fix: **cramming produces recognition, not retention.**

## The forgetting curve

In the 1880s, psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus ran a simple experiment on himself: memorize a list of nonsense syllables, then test his own recall at increasing intervals. The result was the "forgetting curve" — a sharp initial drop in recall within the first day, flattening out over time. Most of what you learn in a single session is gone within 24 hours unless something interrupts that decay.

The interruption that works is a *review*, timed right before you'd otherwise forget. Do that repeatedly, spacing each review a little further apart than the last, and the forgetting curve gets shallower every time. That's spaced repetition in one sentence: **review material at increasing intervals, timed just before you'd forget it.**

## Why this beats re-reading

Re-reading a chapter feels productive because the material feels familiar — but familiarity is a poor proxy for recall. You recognize the words on the page; you haven't tested whether you can produce the answer from memory with the page closed. Spaced repetition forces retrieval (actively recalling an answer), and retrieval practice is consistently one of the most effective study techniques studied in cognitive science, ahead of highlighting, re-reading, and summarizing.

## A practical schedule

You don't need special software to start. A simple version:

1. **Day 0** — learn the material for the first time.
2. **Day 1** — first review (recall, don't re-read).
3. **Day 3** — second review.
4. **Day 7** — third review.
5. **Day 14, then Day 30** — subsequent reviews, doubling the interval each time you recall correctly.

If you get something wrong at any review, reset its interval back to Day 1 — that item clearly hasn't moved into long-term memory yet.

## Where flashcards fit in

Flashcards are the classic delivery mechanism for spaced repetition precisely because each card forces retrieval: you see a term, try to produce the definition from memory, then flip to check. A flashcard deck that's tagged by review date (rather than just shuffled once) is what turns a study aid into an actual spaced-repetition system.
