Most students take notes the same way: write down whatever the slide says, roughly in order, and hope it makes sense later. It usually doesn't. Here's a structure that survives contact with your future, exam-week self.

## Write for retrieval, not transcription

The instinct during a lecture is to transcribe — copy the slide, copy what's said, keep up. But a transcript is not a study resource; it's a wall of text with no signal about what actually matters. Instead, write **questions** in the margin as you go: "why does this happen," "how is this different from X," "what's the formula for this." A page of good questions is worth more at exam time than three pages of copied definitions, because it's already in the format you'll need to study from — retrieval practice, not re-reading.

## Use a two-column structure

A layout that scales from a 50-minute lecture to a 3-hour seminar:

- **Left column (narrow):** keywords, formulas, names — the stuff you'd search for later.
- **Right column (wide):** the actual explanation, examples, and your own paraphrase of what the instructor said.

The left column becomes a self-test: cover the right side, see if the left-column term prompts you to explain it correctly. This is basically a flashcard deck you build in real time.

## Paraphrase, don't quote

If you can copy a sentence verbatim, you weren't thinking about it — you were typing. Force yourself to rephrase every idea in your own words as you write it down. This is slower, which is a feature: it filters out material you didn't actually understand well enough to paraphrase, and flags it for follow-up before the lecture ends rather than three weeks later in a panic.

## Leave a gap for chapter summaries

At the end of each distinct topic (not necessarily each slide), leave a blank line and write one sentence: "In short, this section was about ___." If you can't fill that in cleanly, that's the section to revisit first when you sit down to study.

## Review within 24 hours

Notes decay in usefulness fast if left untouched — abbreviations that made sense mid-lecture stop making sense a week later. A 10-minute pass the same day, turning shorthand into full sentences and flagging anything unclear, is the single highest-leverage thing you can do with lecture notes.
